Social Media Dos and Don’ts for College Students
The Dos

   1. Make your bio work for you — Briefly mention your college, your branch, and a quick line about what you’re into. Think of it as a tiny digital business card for professors or recruiters who might stumble across your profile.
   2. Stick to your real name on professional sites — On platforms like LinkedIn and GitHub, using your actual name makes you much easier to find and helps you start building a professional reputation early.
   3. Pause before hitting post — Before you share something, just ask yourself if you’d be okay with your favorite professor or a future boss seeing it. If it feels a bit risky, it’s probably better to keep it in the drafts.
   4. Show off your wins — Use your feed to talk about the projects you’ve finished, hackathons you’ve joined, or new certifications you’ve earned. It’s a great way to build a public portfolio over time.
   5. Keep your interactions respectful — When you’re commenting or joining a debate, stay thoughtful and give credit where it’s due. It’s always better to avoid getting emotional over controversial topics online.

The Don’ts

   1. Keep your private life private — Never post your phone number, home address, or your daily class schedule. Also, try to avoid sharing your exact location in real-time on public accounts.
   2. Steer clear of offensive content — Avoid posting or sharing anything discriminatory or inflammatory. Remember that even a simple "like" or a repost can be screenshotted and tied back to you later.
   3. Avoid fake accounts — Impersonating someone else or creating fake profiles isn't just against the rules of the platform; it can actually get you into legal trouble under the IT Act.
   4. Don't add just anyone on LinkedIn — Before you hit accept on a connection request from someone you don't know, take a second to verify their profile and company. It’s better to be safe than to share your info with a bot or a fake account.
   5. Don't vent about college publicly — It might be tempting to complain about a tough professor or a frustrating day on campus, but those negative posts can stick around. You don't want to damage professional relationships before you've even graduated.
