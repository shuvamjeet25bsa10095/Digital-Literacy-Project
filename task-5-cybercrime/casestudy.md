Imagine checking your phone and seeing an urgent text from your bank. Your heart sinks—it says your account is locked or your KYC has expired. This is the starting point for a "Fake Bank Alert," a classic phishing scam designed to catch you off guard.
What exactly is Phishing?
Think of it as a digital trap. Instead of hacking a complex computer system, scammers use social engineering to "hack" the person. They pretend to be a trusted source—like your bank or a government agency—to trick you into handing over your passwords, OTPs, or credit card details.
How the Scam Unfolds
It starts with a fake setup. The attacker registers a website name that looks almost identical to the real one, like sbi-secure-alert.com instead of the official sbi.co.in. Then, they send out a wave of emails or SMS messages. These messages create a sense of panic, claiming your money is at risk or your account is about to be suspended.
When you click the link in the message, you’re taken to a site that looks exactly like your bank’s login page. If you enter your ID and password, the scammer captures them in real-time. They might even ask for an OTP to "verify" your identity, which they immediately use to log into your actual account and drain your funds. By the time you see an error message and get redirected to the real site, the damage is already done.
Who is at Risk?
Scammers often target people who are new to digital banking, such as students or the elderly, or anyone who might be expecting a legitimate bank update.
The Aftermath
The impact isn't just financial. Beyond the lost money, victims face the stress of identity theft and a deep sense of shame. Because these funds are moved quickly through a web of accounts, recovering them is incredibly difficult.
